What is CMMC?

Quick Answer

CMMC is the DoD's cybersecurity certification framework requiring all contractors in the Defense Industrial Base to verify — not just self-attest — that they adequately protect government data.

Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) is the cybersecurity framework created by the Department of Defense (DoD) to ensure DoD contractors and subcontractors are properly protecting sensitive government data.

Organizations handling Federal Contract Information (FCI) or Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) must demonstrate compliance at the level required by their contracts.

Unlike previous frameworks, CMMC 2.0 now requires independent third-party assessments for most Level 2 organizations. You can no longer check boxes yourself and call it done.

Key Fact

CMMC requirements are already appearing in active DoD contracts today — not just future ones. If your contract is up for renewal, your CMMC status may already matter.

Do I need CMMC?

Quick Answer

If your organization is part of the DoD supply chain and handles government data, you almost certainly need CMMC. The level required depends on the type of information you handle.

CMMC applies to any organization — prime contractor or subcontractor — that handles Federal Contract Information (FCI) or Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) under a DoD contract.

Many organizations are already under DFARS clause 252.204-7012, which has required NIST 800-171 compliance since 2017. CMMC transforms that self-attestation requirement into a verified, audited standard — meaning the honor system is ending.

If any of these describe your organization, you likely need CMMC:

  • You hold or bid on DoD contracts or subcontracts
  • Your contracts reference DFARS clause 252.204-7012
  • You receive, store, process, or transmit DoD information
  • Information in your systems is marked CUI, FOUO, or sensitive
  • You provide services to a prime contractor on a DoD program

Three documents every CMMC
contractor needs

Quick Answer

The SSP, POA&M, and SPRS score are the three documentation pillars of CMMC compliance. Assessors review all three. RainTech produces all three as part of every engagement.

SSP

System Security Plan

Formally documents how your organization implements each of the 110 NIST SP 800-171 security requirements, mapped to your specific systems, people, and processes.

Why it matters: Your C3PAO assessor reviews the SSP before and during assessment. A generic or incomplete SSP is the most common cause of conditional certifications.

POA&M

Plan of Action & Milestones

Lists your known security gaps, the steps you're taking to remediate them, and realistic timelines for completion. A credible POA&M can allow assessment to proceed under conditional status.

Why it matters: Assessors scrutinize POA&M items closely. Vague timelines or stalled items are red flags that can delay or block certification.

SPRS

SPRS Score

Your score in the Supplier Performance Risk System ranges from −203 to 110 and reflects how many of the 110 NIST 800-171 requirements you currently meet. Contracting officers check this score before awarding contracts.

Why it matters now: No SPRS score is treated as zero — which can disqualify you from contract awards today, not just in 2026.

If a prime contractor handles CUI or FCI, their subcontractors who also handle that information must meet the applicable CMMC level independently. A prime contractor cannot certify on behalf of a subcontractor — each organization is responsible for its own compliance and certification.

CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information) is information that requires safeguarding under law, regulation, or government policy, but is not classified. Common CUI categories include technical data, engineering drawings, defense specifications, export-controlled information (ITAR/EAR), and personally identifiable information related to government programs. If your contracts reference DFARS 252.204-7012, or if information in your systems is marked CUI, FOUO, or sensitive but unclassified (SBU), you almost certainly handle CUI and need CMMC Level 2.

A System Security Plan (SSP) is a formal document that describes how your organization implements each of the 110 NIST SP 800-171 security requirements, mapped to your specific systems, people, and processes. The SSP is one of the primary documents reviewed during a CMMC Level 2 assessment. A generic or incomplete SSP — such as one copied from a template without customization — is one of the most common reasons organizations receive conditional certifications or fail assessments entirely.

A Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) lists your known security weaknesses, the steps you're taking to fix them, and the timeline for completion. In CMMC, a credible POA&M may allow an assessment to proceed under conditional status for items not yet remediated. However, POA&M items are closely scrutinized — assessors look for realistic timelines, owner accountability, and evidence of actual progress. Stalled or vague items are red flags.

For most Level 2 organizations, no — self-attestation is no longer sufficient. A formal assessment by a Certified Third-Party Assessment Organization (C3PAO) is required when CUI is involved. A narrow subset of Level 2 contracts that do not involve CUI may still allow self-assessment, but this must be confirmed by reviewing the specific contract requirements. If you're unsure, assume a C3PAO assessment is required and prepare accordingly.

If you do not pass a CMMC assessment, the C3PAO will issue findings. Depending on the severity, you may receive a conditional certification with a POA&M to address gaps within 180 days, or a failed assessment requiring remediation and a full reassessment. Organizations that work with an RPO before assessment are significantly better prepared and far less likely to encounter findings that delay certification — which is precisely why preparation matters.

DFARS 252.204-7012 is a contract clause that has required DoD contractors to implement NIST SP 800-171 controls and report cyber incidents since 2017 — but compliance was self-attested. CMMC builds on top of DFARS by adding independent verification. Instead of simply claiming compliance, most Level 2 organizations must now have their controls verified by a C3PAO. CMMC effectively ends the honor system that DFARS created and replaces it with an audited standard.

CMMC Terminology

Plain-language definitions for the terms you'll encounter throughout the CMMC compliance process.

CMMC — Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification
The DoD's framework for verifying that contractors in the Defense Industrial Base adequately protect sensitive government information. CMMC 2.0 has three levels.
CUI — Controlled Unclassified Information
Government-created information that requires safeguarding per law or regulation, but is not classified. Includes technical data, export-controlled info, and defense specifications. Handling CUI triggers CMMC Level 2.
FCI — Federal Contract Information
Information provided by or generated for the government under a contract, not intended for public release. Handling FCI triggers CMMC Level 1 at minimum.
RPO — Registered Provider Organization
An organization registered with the Cyber AB to provide CMMC consulting and preparation services. RPOs help contractors scope, prepare, and remediate before a C3PAO conducts the formal assessment.
C3PAO — CMMC Third-Party Assessment Organization
An organization authorized by the Cyber AB to conduct official CMMC Level 2 assessments and issue certifications. Only a C3PAO can certify Level 2 compliance — self-attestation no longer suffices.
SPRS — Supplier Performance Risk System
The DoD database where contractors post their NIST SP 800-171 self-assessment scores (−203 to 110). Contracting officers review SPRS scores before awarding contracts. No score = disqualification risk today.
SSP — System Security Plan
A document that describes how your organization implements each NIST SP 800-171 control. Required for CMMC Level 2. Must be specific to your environment — generic templates do not pass C3PAO assessment.
POA&M — Plan of Action & Milestones
Documents unresolved security weaknesses and the plan to remediate them, including responsible owners and target dates. A credible POA&M may allow conditional assessment status while remediation is in progress.
NIST SP 800-171
A publication by the National Institute of Standards and Technology defining 110 security requirements for protecting CUI in non-federal systems. CMMC Level 2 is built entirely on these 110 controls.
DFARS — Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement
DoD contracting regulations that supplement the FAR. DFARS clause 252.204-7012 has required NIST 800-171 compliance since 2017. CMMC replaces the self-attestation model of DFARS with independent verification.
Cyber AB — CMMC Accreditation Body
The independent accreditation body that oversees the CMMC ecosystem. The Cyber AB registers RPOs, authorizes C3PAOs, and certifies individual assessors (CCA, CCP, CMMC-RP, CMMC-PI).
DIB — Defense Industrial Base
The worldwide industrial complex that enables research, development, and production of military systems and equipment. The DIB includes over 300,000 companies — all of which are subject to CMMC requirements.
